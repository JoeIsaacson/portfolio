Songkick

Fighting scalpers, for the fans.

As design lead of the anti-scalping team, we found a way to get tickets into the hands of the biggest fans of Adele and many other top 100 billboard acts - blocking the sale of 53,000 scalper orders and saving fans over 6.3 million dollars in marked up ticket prices.

Role: Team Design Lead

Goal: Stop scalpers to get real fans the tickets they deserve.

Timeframe: September to December, 2015 (Q4)

The Business Challenge

After working with several artists in the ticket industry pioneering the movement to stop ticket scalping (Eric Church, Mumford and Sons) and building multiple one-off tools for their anti scalping efforts, we began to gain a better understanding of the massive obstacle that is professional ticket scalping - and saw the opportunity in removing scalpers from the equation.

Approximately 60% of prime tickets are purchased through bots, leading to 20% of tickets appearing on the secondary market within 24 hours.

What options do fans have when they are competing against professionals?

Giving fans the best tickets has always been our goal - but our approach has previously been a demand side approach instead of a supply side approach.

“I have never seen more relentless, nefarious and frankly disgusting efforts to defraud the fans and the general public.”
- Eric Church

We knew that unless we were able to secure the ticket supply for the fans and the fans only, they would continually face limited to no options when competing against professional scalpers. Not only where fans losing out on tickets, but they were also paying higher prices while the artist themselves failed to capitalize on the markup of these tickets. The problem was hurting both the artist and the fans directly and indirectly.

Product Goal(s)

In order to achieve a cross product and cross organization goal, we had to divide our product goal into three core focuses:

  1. Goal 1: Identify real fans from scalpers with a registration application that can be used for any and all artists.
  2. Goal 2: Design a seamless access control and purchase experience for one of the largest ticket onsales in UK history.
  3. Goal 3: Develop and design a series of tools that would allow our operations to identify and cancel scalper tools in real time.

For every stage, from the fan facing apps that received 20+ millions impressions, to the internal tools that were used by only a handful of employees, I lead the design of these products to make sure that we hit the sweet spot and combined our efforts to stop scalpers.

Goal 1: How do we identify an artist's fans?

In order to unlock the artist’s ticket inventory, we had to identify the scalpers from the find. It’s the equivalent of finding a needle in a haystack.

Gut checking with a prototype

We started with the basic information from the fan, but had to be discreet as to exactly what we were doing with this information and why. This may come off as dishonest, but if we were to reveal the intent behind collecting the fans data, then our cover (and the artists cover) would be blown.

From there it was a balancing act of creating a convincing user experience for the fan without giving away the true reason of the entire product. Prototyping for contextual feedback was critical in determining key value propositions throughout the flow.

Real Conceptual Feedback

Starting from a simple conceptual level, we had to initially gauge if this was possible from an incentive standpoint. Would fans be convinced to join? And would they be willing to give us the data we requested in the end.

What are people's hesitations around signing up to Facebook? Are they incentivized to sign up?

In the end, we were able to push Facebook sign up from 30% initial testing to 60% and higher.

..and then Contextual Feedback

From there we followed the conceptual prototypes with contextual interviews based on higher fidelity prototypes to get more feedback as to what fans perceptions were when the prototype was embedded in an artist’s website.

Are fans signing up to something controlled by the artist? Or Songkick? Finding the correct balance was key to setting expectations later on in the experience.

...and then again..

By pairing our campaigns to a design sprint, we listed out everything that we assumed about our user experience and listed everything that we wanted to test - per iteration. By adapting the design per campaign, we fell into an iterative groove as we got better sprint-on-sprint.

What can we learn with this upcoming artist on sale? What do we think this artist has in common with our larger artists? What do we think is different?

Working design sprints into campaigns

By embracing a design sprint within a campaign, we were able to iterate on the initial product per campaign - starting with some of our smaller artists, and working our way up to the big fish - Adele.

Goal 2: How do integrate our purchase process?

By running a series of in house usability tests, I simulated a complete, end to end onsale experience from the registration app to the purchase.

This included:

  • 3 fake email templates that were sent to the participants during the session
  • 1 angular app created to simulate the registration app process
  • 1 angular app created to simulate the onsale experience
  • 1 fake credit card

Based on this, we designed a complete experience that spanned several new and existing platforms without creating a jarring user experience.

Goal 3: How do we speed up our internal processes?

In addition to only selling tickets to known fans, there were a series of methods that we had available in order to identify scalper orders and cancel these orders.

Unfortunately, these methods were not scalable.

Our team was faced with a series of time consuming tasks that made the overall process impractical and inaccurate for larger artists. This process was taking on average, 90 to 120 minutes per event, which total was roughly 1,980 - 2,640 hours of work per tour (for an average tour of 22 shows).

What we needed was a streamlined process that required fewer tools involved, fewer steps and less overall time to complete the task.

Measurable product goal

Reduce time spent per event to 30-45 minutes, reducing the required people amount of people to three to cover an onsale consisting of 22 concerts or fewer.

Process

By shadowing one of our employees for several days and she went through the current process, it became evident that there was room for improvement through the consolidation of modification of her set of tools. The products and methodologies that she was currently using was not optimized for this new task at hand.

Identifying the key aspects and then stepping back to identify a solution was beneficial to the incremental product improvement process.

The outcome of the research was a documentation of the current flow and it’s pain point, and also an identification of the features that were (and were not used) throughout her toolset.

Internal tooling prototyping

What followed was a series of prototypes built not in Framer JS or Sketch, but in Looker mySQL reporting and Google Sheets. Using these tools allowed us to make tweaks to the process in real time with little to no developer time needed.

Not the sleekest of prototypes, but this Google spreadsheet proved to reduce the process time by over 20% (+22%.)

The solution was not one feature, but a set of improvements across multiple products. It was a combination of enhancements to our internal tools, communication tools and a table to be returned from our mySql table. Together, this resulted in a 50% reduction in time that it took to complete the task per event.

How did we do?

In the end, we identified scalpers and sold tickets for multiple campaigns, most notable being Adele’s UK and North American tours.

  • Successfully signed up over 35,000 fans through the registration app. Of those fans, we were able to identify enough scalpers to eventually block an estimated 235,000 tickets.
  • Sold over 235,000 tickets total at 1.3x our standard purchase conversion rate.
  • Reduced the anti-scalping process by 50% which allowed us to block over 10,000 scalpers after the onsale.
